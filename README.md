Powerlifting weak spot fix

My project is a tool that assists people in gaining strength in the gym. In particular, it is focused towards the three "lifts" that make up the sport of powerlifting: The Squat; The Bench Press; and the Deadlift. 

As is explained in my about page, an understanding of physics is crucial to lifting heavy weights in the gym. My about page briefly monologues on the purpose of my applicaiton, then my other pages take information from the user, which figures out what their weaknesses are in relation to their lifts.

From there, my application figures out what they need to work on, then gives the user exercises which they should focus on to improve their weaknesses.

Powerlifting is a very simple sport which consists purely of three "lifts": The Squat; The Bench Press; and The Deadlift.

I have always found it to be an extremely satisfying hobby, not just because of the constant goal setting and reaching, but also because it has a lot of interplay with physics. You are moving a barbell with weight from Point A, to Point B. Inevitably, if you add enough weight to the bar, you will no longer be able to move the bar to Point B - it will just be too heavy. When I go to the gym, everyone is making the same mistakes when it comes to increasing their strength. Many people don't get stronger because they just don't eat enough, or aren't doing enough volume (i.e. not doing enough reps per week). However, there is a third key component that is especially important for breaking through what are called plateus or "sticking points". This component is an understanding of the physics of the lifts. 

All can really be boiled down to Isaac Newton, the man who "discovered" gravity. Powerlifting is quite simply you vs gravity, which makes an understanding of Mr Newton's laws of motion essential. 

There are three laws of motion:
1. First Law (Law of Inertia)
An object will stay at rest or keep moving in a straight line at a constant speed unless a force acts on it.

    By far the most important for understanding weak points. In every single lift we do in the gym, this law applies. 

    Think about pushing a car. When the car is stopped, to get it moving requires the most effort. However, once the car starts rolling, it has momentum, and becomes easier to push. This is the same with a barbell. Think about a bench press. You're lying on your back, on a bench, with your arms locked out in front of you, holding the barbell. You lower the barbell until it touches your chest and pause for just a second. This moment, when the bar is stopped on your chest, is the hardest part of the lift. When the bar is stopped on your chest, to push it back to where it was, locked out above your head, you must push against gravity. 

    When the bar is stopped on your chest, this is the static car. Once you start pushing, the bar gains momentum, and it becomes gradually easier to push. 

    In the short term, this means that the hardest part of the lift is when the bar is at the bottom. However, counterintuitively, over time this will actually become your strongest part of the lift, as it has been consistently trained the hardest. That means over time, you will be consistently stronger at the bottom, and fail in the middle or top of the lift. 

    To bypass this, we must change the point where the bar is at the bottom, thereby changing the starting point and changing the point where the lift is the hardest. 

2. Second Law (Force = mass × acceleration)
The force acting on an object equals its mass multiplied by its acceleration (F = ma).

Newton's second law applies again to weak points at the middle or top of a lift. We want to produce force. Force is what moves the bar. If force is mass x acceleration, well then to produce more force, we can either increase the mass, or increase the acceleration. Most people in a gym think to increase mass first, but increasing acceleration whilst lowering mass will produce the same force, and is sometimes the only answer to breaking through a sticking point. 

With Newton's first law, we change the point where the bar starts. With Newton's second law, to train acceleration, we not only consciously try to move the bar faster with lighter weights, but we use two tools in particular to force ourselves to move weight faster. 

The first tool is a chain. Most gyms don't have them, but any strength gym worth their salt will. Think about holding a chain out in front of you and letting it dangle. As you lower it, one link at a time, the chain gets gradually lighter, as more of the chain is lying on the ground, and less of the chain is being held up. If the chain is fully pooled on the ground, it weighs nothing. Then if you gradually lift the chain again, it will slowly get heavier. By hanging chains from a barbell, we can make it so the barbell is heavier at the top, and lighter at the bottom. However, if we lift the barbell fast, we can beat the chain "to the punch", and have the bar in the air before we can feel the effect of the added weight. 

The second tool follows the same principles. It is a resistance band. The more you stretch the resistance band, the more tension pulling the band back. In powerlifting training, we anchor the band to a point on the floor or the squat cage, and lap the other end of the band around the barbell. At the top of the movement, the band should be pulling the bar back towards the ground. At the bottom of the movement, the band shouldn't be loose, but should be slightly slack. Much like the chain, if we move the barbell fast, we can beat the band to the punch. 

3. Third Law (Action and Reaction)
For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

Newton's final law doesn't really apply as much as the first two, but still holds relevance for what we call "form", or in other words, technique. 

It is almost irrelevant for the squat, but most relevant for the deadlift. Many people will pull a deadlift from the ground, which is intuitive, but incorrect. The correct approach is to tighten your core and lat/back muscles, pull the bar horizontally, into your shin, then press your legs into the ground. By pressing, not pulling, you lift a deadlift. The press is the action, the bar lifting from the ground is the opposite reaction. 

This is also true for a bench press. We should push ourselves away from the bar, not the bar away from ourselves, as there is more contact surface with the bench.
